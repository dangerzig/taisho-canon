# Citation Search: Four Claims in Chinese-Tibetan Concordance Paper

Date: 2026-02-27

## Claim 1: 84000 Consulting Chinese Versions

> "Translation projects such as 84000 routinely consult Chinese versions when preparing English translations of Kangyur texts, using the Chinese as a second witness to clarify ambiguous passages."

### Recommended Citation

**84000: Translating the Words of the Buddha, *Guidelines for Translators* (internal document; methodology summarized on 84000.co).**

- 84000's published guidelines state: "Translation should ideally include at least a brief comparison with, and reference to, differing versions when they exist, whether in Tibetan, Chinese, or particularly Sanskrit."
- The organization has staff who "check translations against their source languages (Tibetan, Chinese, and Sanskrit)."
- Individual translations in the 84000 Reading Room document their methodology. For example, the translation of *The Teaching of Aksayamati* (Toh 175) by Braarvig notes consulting Chinese parallels; the translation of *Manjusri's Teaching* (Toh 177) "compared this scripture to the parallel section found in both the Tibetan and Chinese versions of the Catuskaniharasutra."
- Source: https://84000.co/tools-for-translators and individual Reading Room introductions.

**Caveat**: 84000's *Guidelines for Translators* is an internal/quasi-internal document, not a peer-reviewed publication. It has circulated publicly (cached versions exist at docplayer.net) and the methodology is described on the 84000 website, but it is not a traditional scholarly citation. The individual published translations in the Reading Room do constitute published works, and their introductions document the comparative methodology. You may wish to cite a specific 84000 translation that explicitly discusses consulting Chinese witnesses, e.g.:

- 84000: Translating the Words of the Buddha, *The Exposition of Karma* (Toh 338), trans. by the Dharmachakra Translation Committee (84000, 2023), Introduction. (This translation notes using Sanskrit editions in parallel with the Tibetan, choosing Sanskrit readings where the Tibetan was ambiguous.)

**Alternative**: If you want a peer-reviewed source discussing this methodology more broadly:

- Silk, Jonathan A. "Chinese Sutras in Tibetan Translation: A Preliminary Survey." *Annual Report of the International Research Institute for Advanced Buddhology at Soka University* (ARIRIAB) 22 (2019): 227-246.
  - While primarily about Chinese-to-Tibetan translation, Silk's survey discusses the scholarly practice of consulting parallel versions across canons and the understudied nature of cross-canon textual relationships.

## Claim 2: Sanskrit Textual Reconstruction Using Chinese and Tibetan Witnesses

> "Philologists working on Sanskrit textual reconstruction rely on both Chinese and Tibetan translations as independent witnesses to readings in the lost original."

### Recommended Citation 1

**Braarvig, Jens. *Aksayamatinirdesasutra*. 2 vols. Oslo: Solum Forlag, 1993.**

- Vol. I: *Edition of Extant Manuscripts with an Index* (xix + 290 pp.)
- Vol. II: *The Tradition of Imperishability in Buddhist Thought* (cxxxii + 591 pp.)
- The Sanskrit text of this sutra survives only in fragments. It was translated into Chinese five times (two survive) and exists in Tibetan translation. Braarvig's edition reconstructs the text using the surviving Sanskrit fragments together with the Chinese and Tibetan translations as independent witnesses. He argues that the Tibetan translation "can be considered an independent witness, i.e., the Tibetan translators ... made a new translation using the original Sanskrit of the source texts."
- Reviewed in *Bulletin of the School of Oriental and African Studies* (Cambridge).
- This is a classic example of the methodology described in the claim.

### Recommended Citation 2

**Harrison, Paul M. *The Samadhi of Direct Encounter with the Buddhas of the Present: An Annotated English Translation of the Tibetan Version of the Pratyutpanna-Buddha-Sammukhavasthita-Samadhi-Sutra*. Studia Philologica Buddhica, Monograph Series V. Tokyo: The International Institute for Buddhist Studies, 1990. xxxviii + 346 pp.**

- Harrison's work includes a study of the sole surviving fragment of the Sanskrit text, which is "edited, translated, and compared with the corresponding portions of the Chinese and Tibetan versions." The glossary draws material from "all available translations of the work: the Tibetan, the three Chinese versions, and the author's own English rendering."
- This directly demonstrates the philological method of using Chinese and Tibetan translations as witnesses to reconstruct or understand readings in the (mostly lost) Sanskrit original.

## Claim 3: Cross-Canon Data for Tracing Transmission History

> "Historians of Buddhism use cross-canon data to trace the transmission of texts across Central Asia and to establish which texts were available to which Buddhist communities at which periods."

### Recommended Citation 1

**Nattier, Jan. *A Few Good Men: The Bodhisattva Path according to The Inquiry of Ugra (Ugrapariprccha)*. Studies in the Buddhist Traditions. Honolulu: University of Hawai'i Press, 2003. 370 pp.**

- Nattier compares multiple versions of the Ugrapariprccha: Sanskrit fragments, several Chinese translations (spanning centuries), and the Tibetan translation. By analyzing differences across these versions, she traces the sutra's evolution over time and across regions, establishing which textual layers were available to which communities at which periods.
- This is a model study of how cross-canon comparison illuminates transmission history.

### Recommended Citation 2

**Boucher, Daniel. *Bodhisattvas of the Forest and the Formation of the Mahayana: A Study and Translation of the Rastrapalapariprccha-sutra*. Studies in the Buddhist Traditions. Honolulu: University of Hawai'i Press, 2008.**

- Boucher examines the earliest Chinese translation of the Rastrapalapariprccha (by Dharmaraksa, 3rd century) and traces changes through to the late 10th century, comparing against Sanskrit manuscript witnesses. He argues that "the significance of this translation is to be found in the ways it differs from all other witnesses," using cross-canon evidence to establish that "Dharmaraksa's Indic text ... almost certainly came from Greater Gandhara."
- This directly exemplifies historians using cross-canon data to trace text transmission across Central Asia.

### Alternative (if you prefer a more widely known work)

**Nattier, Jan. "The Heart Sutra: A Chinese Apocryphal Text?" *Journal of the International Association of Buddhist Studies* 15, no. 2 (1992): 153-223.**

- Nattier's landmark article uses detailed comparison of Chinese, Tibetan, and Sanskrit versions to argue that the Heart Sutra was composed in Chinese and back-translated into Sanskrit. This is perhaps the most famous example of cross-canon comparison revealing unexpected transmission history.

## Claim 4: Sinologists Locating Tibetan Parallels for Chinese Texts

> "The reverse need, locating Tibetan parallels for Chinese texts, is equally common, and increasingly so as Sinologists working on Chinese Buddhist texts seek to situate them within the broader Indic textual tradition."

### Recommended Citation 1

**Zacchetti, Stefano. *The Da zhidu lun* (Mahaprajnaparamitopadesa) *and the History of the Larger Prajnaparamita*. Hamburg Buddhist Studies 14. Bochum/Freiburg: projektverlag, 2021. 301 pp. Edited for publication by Michael Radich and Jonathan Silk.**

- Zacchetti (a Sinologist specializing in early Chinese Buddhist translations) analyzes the *Da zhidu lun*, a Chinese commentary-translation attributed to Kumarajiva, by systematically comparing it against the broader Prajnaparamita textual tradition preserved in Sanskrit and Tibetan. His method of situating a Chinese Buddhist text within the Indic tradition by reference to Tibetan and Sanskrit parallels directly exemplifies the practice described in the claim.
- His earlier work, *In Praise of the Light: A Critical Synoptic Edition with an Annotated Translation of Chapters 1-3 of Dharmaraksa's Guang zan jing* (Tokyo: IRIAB, 2005), similarly compares the earliest Chinese Prajnaparamita translation against surviving Sanskrit parallels.

### Recommended Citation 2

**Radich, Michael. "Tibetan Evidence for the Sources of Chapters of the Synoptic Suvarnaprabhasottama-sutra T664 Ascribed to Paramartha." *Buddhist Studies Review* 32, no. 2 (2015): 245-270.**

- Radich, working primarily on a Chinese Buddhist text (T664 in the Taisho), uses Tibetan versions of the *Suvarnaprabhasottama-sutra* as evidence to determine the provenance and sources of chapters in the Chinese synoptic edition. He presents evidence that portions of the Tibetan version (called "Tib II") may actually derive from Chinese, despite Tibetan bibliographical claims to the contrary.
- This is a clear example of a scholar working on Chinese Buddhist texts turning to Tibetan parallels to establish textual history. It also shows the bidirectional nature of cross-canon research.
