# Scholarly Sources for Chinese-Tibetan Buddhist Canon Parallels

## Purpose

This document surveys published scholarly work that identifies specific Chinese (Taisho)
to Tibetan (Tohoku/Derge) parallel texts, to supplement the computational concordance
project's current 8 catalog sources (SuttaCentral, 84000 TEI, Lancaster, Lancaster Full,
acmuller Tohoku, CBETA Jinglu Sanskrit, CBETA Jinglu Tibetan, rKTs). The project
currently maps 722 of 2,455 Taisho texts to Tibetan parallels.

## 1. Jonathan A. Silk

### 1a. "Chinese Sutras in Tibetan Translation: A Preliminary Survey"
- **Citation**: Silk, Jonathan A. "Chinese Sutras in Tibetan Translation: A Preliminary
  Survey." *Annual Report of The International Research Institute for Advanced Buddhology
  at Soka University* (ARIRIAB) 22 (2019): 227-246.
- **Available**: [Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/40447816/Chinese_S%C5%ABtras_in_Tibetan_Translation_A_Preliminary_Survey)
- **Content**: Identifies **31 sutras in the Derge Kangyur** that were translated from
  identified Chinese sources. Provides the complete list of Derge numbers:
  D.51, 57, 58, 61, 64, 84, 108, 119, 123, 135, 137, 174, 199, 237, 239, 242, 243,
  256, 264, 335, 336, 341, 351, 353, 354, 359, 555, 691(=897), 692, 694, and 1067.
- **Specific identifications**: ~31 Derge numbers with their Chinese Taisho correspondences
  (including T366 = Amituo jing / D.115 or similar; T374 = Mahaparinirvana / D.119;
  Heart Sutra T251 / D.21 or D.531)
- **Type**: Systematic list with scholarly apparatus
- **Overlap with existing sources**: Many of these 31 are likely already captured by
  Lancaster/CBETA/rKTs (since these are well-known texts), but the systematic
  identification of *all* Chinese-origin sutras in the Kangyur may include some not in
  standard catalogs.
- **Priority**: HIGH. The paper directly provides D-number to T-number mappings. Should
  be checked against existing concordance to identify any gaps.

### 1b. (Related) Li Channa, "A Survey of Tibetan Sutras Translated from Chinese, as
Recorded in Early Tibetan Catalogues"
- **Citation**: Li, Channa. "A Survey of Tibetan Sutras Translated from Chinese, as
  Recorded in Early Tibetan Catalogues." *Revue d'Etudes Tibetaines* 60 (2021): 174-219.
- **Available**: [Digital Himalaya PDF](https://d1i1jdw69xsqx0.cloudfront.net/digitalhimalaya/collections/journals/ret/pdf/ret_60_07.pdf);
  [Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/50977234/A_Survey_of_Tibetan_S%C5%ABtras_Translated_from_Chinese_as_Recorded_in_Early_Tibetan_Catalogues)
- **Content**: Catalogs texts translated from Chinese into Tibetan as recorded in 4 early
  Tibetan catalogs: the Lhan dkar ma (LKK), Phang thang ma (PTK), Bcom ldan ral gri's
  catalog (TGGNO), and Bu ston's catalog (BC). Identifies **23 sutra translations from
  Chinese** found in today's Kangyurs. Also discusses ~40 total texts with some recorded
  only in older catalogs and no longer extant.
- **Specific identifications**: ~23-40 texts with Derge numbers, LKK/PTK catalog entry
  numbers, and Chinese Taisho correspondences.
- **Type**: Systematic tabular list with full scholarly apparatus
- **Overlap**: Significant overlap with Silk 2019 (same topic, later detailed treatment).
  May capture additional texts not in Silk's 31 (e.g., texts recorded in catalogs but
  no longer in the Kangyur, or texts with ambiguous Chinese origins).
- **Priority**: HIGH. The most systematic recent treatment of this specific question.

## 2. Jan Nattier

### 2a. "The Heart Sutra: A Chinese Apocryphal Text?"
- **Citation**: Nattier, Jan. "The Heart Sutra: A Chinese Apocryphal Text?" *Journal of
  the International Association of Buddhist Studies* 15.2 (1992): 153-223.
- **Available**: [JIABS](https://journals.ub.uni-heidelberg.de/index.php/jiabs/article/view/8800);
  [Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/8201316/The_Heart_S%C5%ABtra_A_Chinese_Apocryphal_Text)
- **Content**: Argues that the Heart Sutra was composed in Chinese, with sections
  extracted from Kumarajiva's Large Prajnaparamita (T223). Discusses the relationship
  between T250 (Kumarajiva attribution), T251 (Xuanzang), T223, and the Tibetan
  translation (Toh 21 / Toh 531).
- **Specific identifications**: ~3-5 specific Taisho-Tohoku identifications embedded in
  the argument (T250/T251 to Toh 21/531; T223 to Toh 9).
- **Type**: Occasional identifications within a broader argument
- **Overlap**: These parallels are well known and almost certainly already in your sources.
- **Priority**: LOW (already captured).

### 2b. *A Guide to the Earliest Chinese Buddhist Translations*
- **Citation**: Nattier, Jan. *A Guide to the Earliest Chinese Buddhist Translations:
  Texts from the Eastern Han and Three Kingdoms Periods*. Bibliotheca Philologica et
  Philosophica Buddhica X. Tokyo: IRIAB, Soka University, 2008. 3 Appendixes.
- **Content**: Systematic catalog of the earliest Chinese Buddhist translations (Eastern
  Han through Three Kingdoms, roughly T numbers in the low ranges). For each text,
  provides Taisho number, translator attribution assessment, and parallels in Sanskrit,
  Pali, and Tibetan where known.
- **Specific identifications**: Likely 50-100+ Taisho texts with parallel identifications
  across traditions, though focused on the earliest stratum (approximately T1-T300 range
  plus scattered later texts). Not all will have Tibetan parallels.
- **Type**: Systematic appendix tables
- **Overlap**: Most parallels for major early texts are likely in Lancaster, but Nattier's
  reassessments of attributions and identifications of lesser-known texts may contain
  new information. Her work on Zhi Qian's translations, for instance, identifies
  specific parallels not always noted in standard catalogs.
- **Priority**: MEDIUM-HIGH. The appendix tables should be checked systematically. The
  book is 200+ pages with detailed per-text analysis.

### 2c. *A Few Good Men: The Bodhisattva Path according to The Inquiry of Ugra*
- **Citation**: Nattier, Jan. *A Few Good Men*. Honolulu: University of Hawai'i Press,
  2003.
- **Content**: Study and translation of the Ugrapariprccha. Identifies Chinese versions
  T322, T323, T310(10) with Tibetan Toh 63.
- **Specific identifications**: ~3-4 (T322, T323, T310[10] <-> Toh 63)
- **Type**: Occasional, within a monograph study
- **Overlap**: Well-known text; likely already captured.
- **Priority**: LOW.

### 2d. Various articles on specific text identifications
- Nattier has published detailed studies of individual early Chinese translations that
  often identify Tibetan (and Pali/Sanskrit) parallels. Examples include:
  - "Studies in the Buddhist Translations of Zhi Qian (1): The Abstinence Day Sutra
    (T87)" - identifies Pali and possibly Tibetan parallels
  - Work on the Aksobhyavyuha pureland texts
  - Work on the Candragarbha sutra (comparing Chinese Yuezang jing with Tibetan versions)
- **Specific identifications**: Scattered, perhaps 5-15 across multiple articles
- **Priority**: LOW-MEDIUM. Valuable for individual texts but labor-intensive to extract.

## 3. Paul Harrison

### 3a. *The Tibetan Text of the Pratyutpanna-buddha-sammukhavasthita-samadhi-sutra*
- **Citation**: Harrison, Paul M. Tokyo: Reiyukai Library, 1978 (Studia Philologica
  Buddhica, Monograph Series I).
- **Content**: Critical edition of the Tibetan text. Establishes the correspondence
  between T418 (Pratyutpanna Samadhi Sutra, translated by Lokaksema) and the Tibetan
  Kangyur version (Toh 133).
- **Specific identifications**: T416, T417, T418 <-> Toh 133; plus related texts
- **Type**: Occasional, within a critical edition
- **Overlap**: Likely already captured in Lancaster/CBETA.
- **Priority**: LOW.

### 3b. *Druma-kinnara-raja-pariprccha-sutra* (critical edition)
- **Citation**: Harrison, Paul M. Tokyo: IRIAB, 1992 (Studia Philologica Buddhica,
  Monograph Series IV).
- **Content**: Critical edition of the Tibetan text based on eight Kangyur editions.
  Establishes T624 (Lokaksema) and T625 (Kumarajiva) as Chinese parallels to Toh 157.
- **Specific identifications**: T624, T625 <-> Toh 157
- **Type**: Occasional, within a critical edition
- **Overlap**: Likely already captured.
- **Priority**: LOW.

### 3c. Other work
- Harrison has published extensively on early Mahayana texts with multi-tradition
  comparisons. His work rarely produces *new* text-level identifications not already
  known to catalogers, but does refine understanding of relationships.

## 4. Peter Skilling

### 4a. *Mahasutras: Great Discourses of the Buddha*
- **Citation**: Skilling, Peter. *Mahasutras*. 2 vols. London: Pali Text Society,
  1994-1997 (Sacred Books of the Buddhists, vols. 44, 46).
- **Content**: Critical editions of 10 Tibetan Mahasutras (Sarvastivadin texts in the
  Kangyur) with Pali counterparts. These are protection/paritta texts.
- **Specific identifications**: The 10 Mahasutras each have specific Toh numbers and
  potential Chinese parallels, though Skilling focuses primarily on Pali-Tibetan
  comparison. Chinese parallels exist for some (e.g., Caturdharmasutra = T773;
  Dhvajagra sutra parallels in Chinese).
- **Type**: ~10 specific Tibetan-Chinese identifications possible
- **Overlap**: Most of these parallels should be in standard catalogs already.
- **Priority**: LOW-MEDIUM.

### 4b. *Questioning the Buddha: A Selection of Twenty-Five Sutras*
- **Citation**: Skilling, Peter. *Questioning the Buddha*. Somerville, MA: Wisdom
  Publications, 2021.
- **Content**: Translations of 25 sutras with comparative notes across traditions.
  Contains Chinese and Tibetan parallel references for each sutra.
- **Specific identifications**: ~25 texts with cross-tradition references
- **Overlap**: Standard texts, likely already captured.
- **Priority**: LOW.

## 5. Adelheid Herrmann-Pfandt

### 5a. *Die lHan kar ma: Ein fruher Katalog der ins Tibetische ubersetzten
buddhistischen Texte*
- **Citation**: Herrmann-Pfandt, Adelheid. *Die lHan kar ma*. Vienna: Verlag der
  Osterreichischen Akademie der Wissenschaften, 2008. (BKGA 59)
- **Content**: First critical edition of the 9th-century lHan kar ma (Denkarma) catalog
  (~750 titles of Tibetan translations). For each entry, provides an apparatus including
  numbers of text versions in Dunhuang collections, the 'Phang thang ma, Bu ston's
  catalog, and **Tibetan and Chinese editions of the Buddhist canon** (including
  Taisho/Nanjio cross-references where identifiable).
- **Specific identifications**: Potentially **hundreds** of cross-references between
  lHan kar ma entries and Taisho numbers, depending on how systematically the Chinese
  correspondences are provided.
- **Type**: Systematic apparatus for each of ~750 entries
- **Overlap**: The lHan kar ma entries that have known Taisho correspondences should
  largely overlap with Lancaster and CBETA, but Herrmann-Pfandt's critical apparatus
  may identify correspondences not in standard catalogs, especially for obscure or
  disputed texts.
- **Priority**: HIGH. This is a major scholarly resource that could contain dozens of
  identifications not in our current sources. The difficulty is that it is a 500+ page
  German-language publication requiring physical access.

## 6. Paul G. Hackett

### 6a. *A Catalogue of the Comparative Kangyur*
- **Citation**: Hackett, Paul G. *A Catalogue of the Comparative Kangyur (bka' 'gyur dpe
  bsdur ma)*. Treasury of the Buddhist Sciences. New York: American Institute of
  Buddhist Studies / Columbia University Press, 2013.
- **Content**: Detailed catalog of the Comparative (dpe bsdur ma) Kangyur recension.
  Includes concordance tables aligning catalog numbers between 7 Kangyur recensions
  (Derge, Peking, Narthang, Cone, Litang, Urga, and the Comparative itself), plus
  indices to Tibetan and Sanskrit titles, translators, and revisers.
- **Specific identifications**: The catalog provides Tibetan inter-recension concordances
  but **does NOT appear to include systematic Taisho cross-references**. It focuses on
  aligning different Tibetan recensions with each other, not on Chinese parallels.
- **Type**: Tibetan-internal concordance (Toh <-> Peking <-> Narthang, etc.)
- **Overlap**: N/A for Chinese parallels
- **Priority**: LOW for Taisho-Tohoku mapping purposes. The Otani (Peking) numbers it
  provides may supplement what rKTs already gives you.

## 7. AIBS Buddhist Canons Research Database

- **Citation**: American Institute of Buddhist Studies. "Buddhist Canons Research
  Database." Columbia University. http://databases.aibs.columbia.edu/
- **Content**: Bibliographic information for ~5,000 texts in the Tibetan Buddhist canon
  (Kangyur + Tengyur). Includes Tibetan titles, Sanskrit titles, translators, and
  cross-links. The database appears to focus primarily on the Tibetan canon rather than
  providing systematic Taisho cross-references.
- **Specific identifications**: Unknown; the database was not accessible during this
  review. It may contain Chinese cross-references for some texts, but this does not
  appear to be a primary feature.
- **Priority**: MEDIUM. Worth investigating if the database can be queried for Chinese
  cross-references.

## 8. Digital Humanities / Computational Approaches

### 8a. DharmaNexus / Dharmamitra (Sebastian Nehrdich et al.)
- **Citation**: Nehrdich, Sebastian, et al. "MITRA: A Large-Scale Parallel Corpus and
  Multilingual Pretrained Language Model for Machine Translation and Semantic Retrieval
  for Pali, Sanskrit, Buddhist Chinese, and Tibetan." arXiv:2601.06400 (2025).
- **Platform**: https://dharmamitra.org/nexus
- **Content**: DharmaNexus is an interactive database for exploring intertextual
  connections within and between texts in Pali, Sanskrit, Chinese, and Tibetan. Uses
  MITRA, a 1.74 million parallel sentence pair corpus. Operates at the **passage level**
  (highlighting similar passages between texts) rather than providing catalog-level
  cross-references.
- **Specific identifications**: Does not provide text-level Taisho-Tohoku identifications
  directly. However, the underlying passage-level matching could in principle be
  aggregated to identify text-level parallels not in existing catalogs.
- **Type**: Passage-level, not catalog-level
- **Overlap**: Different approach from catalog concordance work
- **Priority**: MEDIUM-LONG-TERM. The passage-level approach could discover entirely new
  parallels but requires significant post-processing to extract text-level
  identifications. Could be explored in a future phase.

### 8b. "Crosslinguistic Semantic Textual Similarity of Buddhist Chinese and Classical
Tibetan"
- **Citation**: Nehrdich, Sebastian, and Oliver Hellwig. "Crosslinguistic Semantic
  Textual Similarity of Buddhist Chinese and Classical Tibetan." *Journal of Open
  Humanities Data* (2022). DOI: 10.5334/johd.86
- **Content**: Pilot study using cross-lingual embedding spaces to identify similar
  passages between Chinese and Tibetan Buddhist texts. Tested on 3 Chinese sutras from
  the Maharatnakuta collection.
- **Specific identifications**: Methodology paper; does not provide new text-level IDs.
- **Priority**: LOW (methodology interest only).

### 8c. Michael Radich's TACL Tool
- **Citation**: Radich, Michael, and Jamie Norrish. "TACL: Text Analysis for Corpus
  Linguistics." Available at https://dazangthings.nz/
- **Content**: N-gram comparison tool for the Chinese Buddhist canon. Primarily used for
  translator attribution and intertextual analysis *within* the Chinese canon (not
  cross-canon). The "Radich Taisho corpus" provides a modified CBETA corpus for
  computational analysis.
- **Specific identifications**: Not directly relevant for Chinese-Tibetan cross-refs, but
  could be combined with Tibetan corpus tools for cross-canon work.
- **Priority**: LOW for this project's immediate needs.

## 9. Helmut Eimer

- **Citation**: Eimer, Helmut. Various publications on Kangyur stemma and manuscript
  catalogs (1980s-2000s).
- **Content**: Eimer is the leading scholar on Kangyur textual genealogy and has published
  location lists and catalogs for various Kangyur manuscripts (Bathang, Phug brag, etc.).
  His work focuses on the **internal history of the Tibetan canon** rather than on
  Chinese parallels.
- **Specific identifications**: Does not appear to provide systematic Taisho
  cross-references.
- **Priority**: LOW for Taisho-Tohoku mapping purposes.

## 10. Kawagoe Eishin (Phangthangma)

- **Citation**: Kawagoe Eishin. *Dkar chag 'Phang thang ma*. Sendai, 2005.
- **Content**: Critical edition of the Phangthangma catalog (one of three 9th-century
  Tibetan imperial catalogs, alongside the Denkarma).
- **Specific identifications**: The catalog itself lists Tibetan translations; Chinese
  cross-references would depend on Kawagoe's editorial apparatus. The Phangthangma
  records some texts translated from Chinese.
- **Overlap**: The Chinese-origin texts in the Phangthangma are largely a subset of those
  in the Denkarma (both being imperial-era catalogs).
- **Priority**: MEDIUM. Should be compared with Herrmann-Pfandt 2008 and Li Channa 2021
  for any additional identifications.

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Download and extract Li Channa 2021** from Revue d'Etudes Tibetaines. This is
   freely available and likely the single most efficient source of new identifications.
   Check each of the ~40 texts against the existing concordance.

2. **Download and extract Silk 2019** from Academia.edu. Cross-reference the 31 Derge
   numbers against your existing Tohoku mappings to identify any gaps.

3. **Investigate Nattier 2008** appendix tables if accessible. The earliest Chinese
   translations (Eastern Han / Three Kingdoms) are an area where standard catalogs may
   have gaps, especially for texts with uncertain attributions.

4. **Consider Herrmann-Pfandt 2008** as a longer-term resource. The Chinese cross-
   references in the Denkarma critical apparatus could contain substantial new
   identifications, but extracting them requires German-language access and significant
   manual effort.

5. **Monitor DharmaNexus** for future capability to export text-level parallel
   identifications. The 1.74M sentence-pair corpus could eventually reveal entirely
   new cross-canon parallels that no scholar has previously identified.

## Key Finding: The 31 Chinese-Origin Sutras in the Derge Kangyur

The most important concrete finding from this search is the complete list of Derge
numbers for sutras translated from Chinese (from Silk 2019 / Li 2021):

D.51, 57, 58, 61, 64, 84, 108, 119, 123, 135, 137, 174, 199, 237, 239, 242, 243,
256, 264, 335, 336, 341, 351, 353, 354, 359, 555, 691(=897), 692, 694, 1067

Each of these 31 texts should have a known Chinese Taisho source. If any of these
Derge-to-Taisho mappings are missing from the current concordance, they represent
definite additions from published scholarship.

Notable specific correspondences confirmed through this research:
- D.119 = T374/T375 (Mahaparinirvana-sutra)
- D.115 (or similar) = T366 (Amitabha Sutra, Kumarajiva)
- D.21 / D.531 = T250/T251 (Heart Sutra)
- D.157 = T624/T625 (Druma-kinnara-raja-pariprccha)
- D.133 = T418 (Pratyutpanna Samadhi Sutra)
- D.63 = T322/T323 (Ugrapariprccha)
- D.555/556/557 = T663/T664/T665 (Suvarnaprabhasottama, Golden Light Sutra)
