# Journal Recommendations

## Paper: "Computational Detection of Text Reuse in the Chinese Buddhist Canon"

**Paper profile:** ~5,700 words (excluding notes/bibliography); 5-stage computational pipeline for detecting text reuse across the full Taisho Tripitaka (~2,920 texts); 3,610 relationships detected; phonetic transliteration module; Heart Sutra validation; Tibetan parallel cross-validation. Fundamentally a computational/digital humanities methodology paper with deep Buddhist Studies content.

**Constraint:** Cannot target JIABS (already used for the Tibetan concordance paper).

---

## Tier 1: Strongest Fit

### 1. Digital Scholarship in the Humanities (DSH)

**Publisher:** Oxford University Press (on behalf of EADH/ADHO)
**Why it fits:** DSH is the flagship journal for digital humanities methodology. The paper's pipeline architecture, algorithmic approach (n-gram fingerprinting, Smith-Waterman-style alignment, classification scoring), and corpus-scale analysis are exactly the kind of work DSH publishes. The 2025 Bingenheimer et al. article on topic modeling Chinese Buddhist texts in DHQ shows there is active interest in computational Buddhist studies, and DSH is the higher-prestige venue in this space.

- **Word limit:** Long papers up to 6,000 words (notes and bibliography excluded). The current ~5,700-word paper fits within this limit.
- **Important caveat:** DSH states it "does not publish work-in-progress reports, descriptions of projects, tools, techniques or methodologies." The paper must frame itself around theoretical contributions and completed results, not as a pipeline description. The current emphasis on the findings (encyclopedic absorption patterns, dharani networks, Vinaya derivation chains) rather than just the tool itself is the right approach.
- **Review timeline:** Not publicly stated; anecdotally 4-8 months.
- **Open access:** Hybrid (subscription default; OA available with APC, often covered by institutional read-and-publish agreements with OUP).
- **Impact factor:** 1.1 (Clarivate 2024); CiteScore 2.5 (Scopus 2024). Highest-impact DH journal.
- **Similar published work:** Computational analysis of Chinese and English texts; text reuse detection in historical corpora; stylometric analysis of classical texts.
- **Recommendation:** Top choice. Reframe the paper to lead with the scholarly findings and theoretical implications of text reuse in the Chinese canon, with the pipeline as the instrument that enables those findings.

### 2. Digital Humanities Quarterly (DHQ)

**Publisher:** Alliance of Digital Humanities Organizations (ADHO) / Association for Computers and the Humanities (ACH)
**Why it fits:** DHQ published Bingenheimer, Brody, and Nichols, "Experiments in Distant Reading: Using Topic Modeling on Chinese Buddhist Texts from 500-800 CE" in 2025 (Vol. 19, No. 1). This is an extremely close precedent: computational methods applied to the CBETA corpus of Chinese Buddhist texts. The paper would be joining a demonstrated publication track at DHQ.

- **Word limit:** No fixed word limit. The journal advises that "reader attention is not unbounded" and asks that submissions merit their length.
- **Review timeline:** ~24 weeks (6 months) from submission to publication. Rolling quarterly deadlines: Jan 15, Apr 15, Jul 15, Oct 15.
- **Open access:** Fully open access (Diamond OA, no author fees). Published under CC-BY-ND by default; authors may choose CC-BY or CC0.
- **Impact factor:** 0.82 (Clarivate 2024, rising trend); Q1 in Journal Citation Indicator. Recently accepted for Scopus indexing.
- **Similar published work:** Bingenheimer et al. (2025) on topic modeling Chinese Buddhist texts is a direct precedent.
- **Recommendation:** Excellent fit with proven receptivity to Buddhist DH work. Lower prestige than DSH but fully open access, no word limit constraints, and a demonstrated track record with this exact subject area. The single-blind (not double-blind) review may also work in the author's favor given the specialized nature of the work.

## Tier 2: Strong Fit

### 3. Computational Humanities Research (CHR)

**Publisher:** Cambridge University Press
**Why it fits:** CHR is a new journal (launched 2024) explicitly positioned at "the intersection of computational methods and the humanities." It publishes transdisciplinary papers grounded in humanities research questions that use computational, quantitative methodologies. The paper's pipeline architecture, classification taxonomy, and large-scale corpus analysis fit the journal's stated scope precisely.

- **Word limit:** Conference papers are 3,000-5,000 words; journal article limits not yet clearly stated but likely longer. The IMRaD structure requirement for empirical research articles is notable and would require some restructuring.
- **Review timeline:** Not yet established (new journal).
- **Open access:** Gold Open Access (no APC for authors).
- **Impact factor:** None yet (too new for indexing). However, the Cambridge University Press brand carries significant weight.
- **Similar published work:** Various computational corpus analysis papers; the journal is actively building its catalog.
- **Recommendation:** High potential for a computationally sophisticated paper. The newness of the journal is both a risk (no established IF) and an opportunity (less competition, editorial appetite for strong papers). The required IMRaD structure would mean reorganizing the paper to separate methods from results more clearly. Consider if DSH or DHQ do not work out.

### 4. Journal of Data Mining and Digital Humanities (JDMDH)

**Publisher:** Episciences (French open-access platform)
**Why it fits:** JDMDH sits at the intersection of data mining and digital humanities. The paper's n-gram fingerprinting, candidate selection, alignment scoring, and classification pipeline are fundamentally data mining operations applied to a humanities corpus. The phonetic transliteration detection module adds a novel NLP dimension.

- **Word limit:** Not publicly stated; appears flexible.
- **Review timeline:** ~8 weeks from submission to publication (remarkably fast).
- **Open access:** Diamond Open Access (no author fees, no APC). CC-BY license.
- **Impact factor:** Not indexed in Web of Science (no Clarivate IF). Published 26 articles in 2024. Lower prestige but growing.
- **Similar published work:** Text mining of historical corpora; NLP applications to cultural heritage datasets.
- **Recommendation:** The fastest publication path by far (8 weeks). Diamond OA means no cost. The trade-off is lower prestige and no established impact factor. Good choice if speed matters or as a fallback.

### 5. Language Resources and Evaluation (LRE)

**Publisher:** Springer
**Why it fits:** LRE publishes work on language resources and computational tools for processing them. The CBETA corpus as a language resource, the text extraction and normalization pipeline, the phonetic transliteration table built from the DDB dictionary, and the classification framework all speak to LRE's core interests. The journal has published work on text reuse detection and on computational text analysis within the humanities.

- **Word limit:** 18-25 pages (full-length); approximately 8,000-12,000 words.
- **Review timeline:** Not publicly stated; Springer journals typically 3-6 months.
- **Open access:** Hybrid. APC for OA: $3,490 USD / 2,790 EUR. Subscription publishing (no APC) also available.
- **Impact factor:** 1.8 (Clarivate 2024); CiteScore 4.3 (Scopus). Q1 ranking.
- **Important caveat:** LRE asks that "resources described in publications be made publicly available." This means the pipeline code and possibly the processed corpus data would need to be shared. LRE also requires strong English editing and may return papers that do not meet language standards.
- **Similar published work:** "Computational text analysis within the Humanities" (2019); cross-lingual text reuse detection; NLP for historical texts.
- **Recommendation:** Good fit for the technical/computational aspects, with a higher impact factor than most DH journals. The paper would need to emphasize the language resource and evaluation dimensions (the corpus processing pipeline, the phonetic table, the classification benchmarks) more than the Buddhist Studies findings. The word limit (18-25 pages) would allow a more expanded treatment. Consider if you want the paper positioned more toward computational linguistics than digital humanities.

## Tier 3: Possible but Less Ideal

### 6. Journal of the Japanese Association for Digital Humanities (JJADH)

**Publisher:** Japanese Association for Digital Humanities (hosted on J-STAGE)
**Why it fits:** JJADH has published Bingenheimer's stylometric analysis of Chinese Buddhist texts (2017) and a special issue on "Buddhism and Technology." The journal has a specific track record with computational Buddhist Studies work and an audience familiar with both East Asian texts and digital methods.

- **Word limit:** 4,000-8,000 words (10-18 pages).
- **Review timeline:** Double-blind peer review; timeline not publicly stated.
- **Open access:** Fully open access (hosted on J-STAGE).
- **Impact factor:** No Clarivate IF; very low citation metrics (45% of articles uncited). Niche audience.
- **Similar published work:** Bingenheimer (2017) on stylometric analysis of Chinese Buddhist texts; Buddhism and Technology special issue.
- **Recommendation:** Perfect subject-matter fit but very limited reach and impact. Best considered if you specifically want to reach the Japanese DH community or if other options are exhausted. The paper may be slightly long for their upper word limit depending on final length.

### 7. International Journal of Digital Humanities (IJDH)

**Publisher:** Springer
**Why it fits:** IJDH covers "digital media and the development, application and reflection of digital research methodology in the Humanities." The computational pipeline and its application to the Chinese Buddhist canon fit squarely within scope.

- **Word limit:** 30,000-50,000 characters including spaces (roughly 5,000-8,500 words).
- **Review timeline:** Not publicly stated.
- **Open access:** Hybrid. APC for OA: $3,190 USD / 2,590 EUR. Subscription publishing also available.
- **Impact factor:** Very low (21% of articles uncited; limited citation data). Relatively new journal.
- **Similar published work:** Historical document analysis; handwritten text recognition for digitized sources.
- **Recommendation:** Acceptable backup option. The Springer platform provides good indexing and discoverability, but the journal has not yet established strong impact metrics. The APC is expensive for a journal without demonstrated reach.

## Not Recommended (with reasons)

### JASPA (Journal of the American Society for Premodern Asia, formerly JAOS)
The journal was renamed from JAOS in 2025 and will publish under the new name starting with Volume 126 (2026). While it is the premier American Orientalist journal, it has virtually no track record with computational or digital humanities methodology papers. Impact factor is only 0.11. The readership expects philological and historical scholarship, not pipeline architecture. The paper's computational methods would likely alienate reviewers, and the Buddhist Studies content, while substantial, is presented in service of the methodology rather than as standalone philological argument.

### T'oung Pao
The leading international journal for traditional Chinese studies (founded 1890, published by Brill). Extremely prestigious in Sinology but fundamentally a philological journal. Impact factor 0.2. No track record with computational methods. Articles typically involve close textual analysis, historical argumentation, and deep engagement with Chinese-language scholarship. The paper's computational approach would be a poor fit for the journal's editorial culture and reviewer pool.

### Buddhist Studies Review
Published by Equinox for the UK Association for Buddhist Studies. Very low impact (CiteScore 0.2-0.5). No apparent track record with digital humanities or computational methods. Word limit information not publicly available. The journal serves a traditional Buddhist Studies audience that would be more interested in the findings than the methodology, but the findings are presented in a way that foregrounds the computational approach.

### Asia Major
Published by Academia Sinica's Institute of History and Philology. Prestigious in Sinology but, like T'oung Pao, oriented toward traditional philological and historical scholarship. No digital humanities track record.

### Journal of Chinese Religions
Published by Taylor and Francis for the Society for the Study of Chinese Religions. Oriented toward religious studies rather than computational methods. No DH track record. The paper's Buddhist content is incidental to the text reuse methodology.

## Recommended Strategy

**Primary target: DSH.** The paper's current length (~5,700 words) fits within the 6,000-word limit. The key revision task is to ensure the paper reads as a scholarly contribution about text reuse in the Chinese canon (with the pipeline as instrument) rather than as a technical report describing the pipeline. DSH explicitly rejects "descriptions of projects, tools, techniques or methodologies" that lack theoretical depth.

**Backup: DHQ.** If DSH rejects, DHQ is the natural second choice. The Bingenheimer et al. (2025) precedent demonstrates editorial receptivity to Chinese Buddhist computational analysis. No word limit means the paper could be expanded to include more Buddhist Studies context. Fully open access with no fees.

**Alternative positioning: LRE.** If you want to position the paper toward computational linguistics rather than digital humanities, LRE offers the highest impact factor (1.8) and a broader technical audience. This would require expanding the paper (18-25 pages) and reframing it around the corpus processing pipeline, the phonetic transliteration resource, and evaluation benchmarks.
