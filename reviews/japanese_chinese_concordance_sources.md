# Japanese and Chinese Scholarly Sources for Taisho-Tibetan Concordances

## Research Report: February 2026 (revised 2026-02-26)

This report surveys Japanese and Chinese language scholarship, as well as
digital humanities projects, that provide systematic cross-references or
concordance tables between Taisho numbers (T-numbers) and Tibetan Buddhist
canon numbers (Tohoku/Derge). It is organized by priority and likely yield of
new identifications beyond our existing sources. The initial version focused on
print scholarship; this revision adds digital databases and computational tools
discovered through targeted web searches for East Asian and multilingual
resources.

---

## 1. Hobogirin Repertoire (Fascicule Annexe) -- HIGH PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
Paul Demieville, Hubert Durt, and Anna Seidel, eds. *Repertoire du Canon
Bouddhique Sino-Japonais: Edition de Taisho (Taisho Shinshu Daizokyo)*,
2nd ed., rev. and enlarged. Paris/Tokyo: Academie des Inscriptions et
Belles-Lettres / Maison Franco-Japonaise, 1978. 372 pp.
ISBN: 9782720001888.

**Original:** Sylvain Levi and Takakusu Junjiro, eds. *Hobogirin:
Dictionnaire Encyclopedique du Bouddhisme d'apres les Sources Chinoises et
Japonaises. Fascicule Annexe: Tables du Taisho Issaikyo*. Tokyo: Maison
Franco-Japonaise, 1931. 202 pp.

**What it contains:**
The Repertoire is an exhaustive index to every text in Taisho volumes 1-85.
Table I (pp. 1-126 in the 1931 ed.; greatly expanded in 1978) lists for each
Taisho text: Chinese title with Japanese and Mandarin readings, number of
fascicles, Sanskrit/Pali title, author/translator, concordance numbers to other
Chinese canon editions, and related-work cross-references. The 1978 edition
restored Sanskrit titles from Tibetan documents (marked with a double-cross
symbol), meaning Tibetan equivalents are systematically noted where known.

**Digital availability:**
The Digital Hobogirin is online at https://tripitaka.l.u-tokyo.ac.jp/hbgrn/
(University of Tokyo), released in 2021 in collaboration with the Academie des
Inscriptions et Belles-Lettres. It is searchable by map, timeline, and text
fields.

**Estimated new identifications:** Potentially 100-300+ Taisho-Tibetan
correspondences beyond what we have, depending on the degree to which the 1978
edition systematically includes Tohoku/Otani/Peking numbers. The 1931 edition
predated the Tohoku catalogue (1934), but the 1978 revision incorporated later
scholarship. The digital version should make this data extractable.

**Assessment:** This is likely the single most valuable untapped source. The
comprehensive coverage of all Taisho texts with cross-references to Tibetan
equivalents where known makes this essential. The digital version may allow
programmatic data extraction.

## 2. Tohoku Catalogue and Supplements -- HIGH PRIORITY (partial overlap)

### 2a. Original Tohoku Catalogue (1934)

**Full citation:**
Ui Hakuju, Suzuki Munetada, Kanakura Yensho, and Tada Tokan, eds.
*Chibetto Daizokyo Somokuroku / A Complete Catalogue of the Tibetan Buddhist
Canons (Bkah-hgyur and Bstan-hgyur)*. Sendai: Tohoku Imperial University
(aided by the Saito Gratitude Foundation), 1934.

**What it contains:**
The first complete catalogue of all 4,569 texts in the Derge edition of the
Tibetan Buddhist canon (Toh 1-1108 Kangyur, 1109-4569 Tengyur). Each entry
includes: Tibetan title, Sanskrit title where available, author/translator,
location in the Derge edition, and Chinese equivalent references where known.

**Digital availability:**
- Available as PDF on Internet Archive (BDRC): https://library.bdrc.io/show/bdr:MW19855
- Available on Scribd: https://www.scribd.com/document/117820160/
- Also reproduced at http://prajnaquest.fr/blog/tohoku-catalogue-available-here/

**Estimated new identifications:** The original 1934 catalogue does include
Chinese canon cross-references for many entries, but these use the Nanjio
numbering system (predating Taisho) rather than T-numbers directly. A
systematic extraction of these Nanjio-to-Toh mappings, converted via
Nanjio-to-Taisho concordance, could yield a substantial number of
correspondences. However, many of these may already be captured by rKTs and
CBETA Jinglu. Best value would come from entries not yet in our database.

### 2b. Catalogue-Index (1953)

**Full citation:**
Kanakura Yensho, Yamada Ryujo, Tada Tokan, and Hadano Hakuyu, eds.
*A Catalogue of the Tohoku University Collection of Tibetan Works on
Buddhism*. Sendai: Seminary of Indology, Tohoku University, 1953.

**What it contains:**
Supplementary catalogue covering the broader Tohoku University collection of
Tibetan Buddhist works beyond the Derge Kangyur/Tengyur. Includes non-canonical
Tibetan Buddhist texts held at Tohoku.

**Estimated new identifications:** Limited for our purposes, as the
supplement covers non-canonical texts that are less likely to have Taisho
parallels.

## 3. Otani University Peking Tripitaka Catalogues -- HIGH PRIORITY

### 3a. Comparative Analytical Catalogue of the Kanjur Division

**Full citation:**
*A Comparative Analytical Catalogue of the Kanjur Division of the Tibetan
Tripitaka, Edited in Peking during the K'ang-hsi Era, Kept in the Library of
the Otani Daigaku Kyoto*. 3 vols. Kyoto: Otani Daigaku Library, 1930-1932.

### 3b. Comparative Analytical Catalogue of the Tanjur Division

**Full citation:**
*A Comparative Analytical Catalogue of the Tanjur Division of the Tibetan
Tripitaka Kept in the Otani University Library*. 9 vols. Kyoto, 1965-1997.

### 3c. Catalogue & Index (Suzuki edition)

**Full citation:**
D.T. Suzuki, ed. *The Tibetan Tripitaka, Peking Edition, Kept in the Library
of the Otani University, Kyoto: Catalogue & Index*. Vols. 165-168 of the
photographic reprint. Tokyo: Tibetan Tripitaka Research Institute, 1955-1961.

**What these contain:**
The Otani catalogues provide systematic comparative analysis of the Peking
edition texts, including cross-references to the Derge edition and to Chinese
equivalents (including Taisho numbers in the later volumes). The "Comparative
Analytical" in the title refers specifically to the comparative study across
editions, including Chinese canon correspondences.

**Digital availability:**
Otani University maintains the Peking Tripitaka Online Search at
https://web.otani.ac.jp/cri/twrpe/peking/ which provides searchable access
to the Peking Kanjur and Tanjur with concordance to Peking, Derge, and Golden
Manuscript Tanjur page numbers.

**Estimated new identifications:** The Otani catalogues are among the most
systematic Japanese concordance works. While many of these correspondences may
already be captured by rKTs (which draws on them), the Tanjur catalogue
(1965-1997) in particular may contain correspondences for commentarial
literature not well covered by other sources. Possibly 50-200 additional
correspondences for Tanjur texts.

## 4. Lancaster/Muller Digital Descriptive Catalogue -- MODERATE PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
Lewis R. Lancaster, in collaboration with Sung-bae Park. *The Korean
Buddhist Canon: A Descriptive Catalogue*. Berkeley: University of California
Press, 1979. Digital edition by A. Charles Muller at
http://www.acmuller.net/descriptive_catalogue/

**What it contains:**
We already use Lancaster 1979, but Muller's digital edition provides additional
cross-reference indexes not easily accessible in the print version:
- **Tohoku Index**: http://www.acmuller.net/descriptive_catalogue/indexes/index-tohoku.html
  Maps Toh numbers to K-numbers (and thus to T-numbers via the Taisho Index)
- **Taisho Index**: http://www.acmuller.net/descriptive_catalogue/indexes/index-taisho.html
  Direct T-number to K-number mapping
- **Nanjio Index**: http://www.acmuller.net/descriptive_catalogue/indexes/index-nanjio.html

The combination of the Tohoku Index and Taisho Index provides a direct
Toh-to-T mapping mediated through K-numbers.

**Estimated new identifications:** Since we already use Lancaster, the
marginal yield is in the digital cross-referencing capability. The Tohoku
Index likely contains several hundred Toh-to-K mappings. Any entries where
the K-number also has a T-number could fill gaps in our current concordance.
Possibly 50-100 additional identifications from systematic extraction.

## 5. Kawagoe Eishin's Phangthangma Study -- MODERATE PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
Kawagoe Eishin (川越英真). *Dkar chag 'Phang thang ma*. Sendai: Tohoku
Indo-Chibetto Kenkyukai (東北インド・チベット研究会), 2005.

Also: Kawagoe Eishin. "A Study of dKar chag 'Phang thang ma." *Japanese
Association for Tibetan Studies* (Nihon Chibetto Gakkai), May 2005.
CiNii: https://ci.nii.ac.jp/naid/110006164661

**What it contains:**
A critical study and edition of the Phangthangma catalogue, one of three
historically attested Tibetan imperial catalogues (9th century) listing
translations of Sanskrit Buddhist texts into Tibetan. The Phangthangma
predates the standardized Kangyur/Tengyur division and provides early evidence
for which texts were translated and by whom.

**Estimated new identifications:** The Phangthangma itself lists early
Tibetan translations, many of which correspond to texts in the Chinese canon.
Kawagoe's study likely identifies some of these correspondences. However, the
texts listed in the Phangthangma are generally also in the later Derge edition
(and thus already catalogued via Toh numbers). The primary value is for
historical context and for identifying texts that were translated from Chinese
into Tibetan during the imperial period. Possibly 10-30 additional
identifications, primarily for texts translated from Chinese.

## 6. ICPBS Taisho Canon Concordance Series -- HIGH PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
International College for Postgraduate Buddhist Studies Library (国際仏教学
大学院大学図書館), Tokyo.

Published volumes:
- **Volume I**: *A Concordance to the Taisho Canon and the Zhonghua Canon
  (Beijing Edition)* (大正蔵・中華蔵（北京版）対照目録), 2004.
- **Volume II**: *A Concordance to the Taisho Canon and Dunhuang Buddhist
  Manuscripts* (大正蔵・敦煌出土仏典対照目録), 3rd provisional ed., 2015.
  By Sueki Fumihiko et al. XXVII + 658 pp.
- **Volume III**: *A Concordance to the Taisho Canon and Nine Other Buddhist
  Canons: Preliminary Edition*.

**URL:** https://www.icabs.ac.jp/en/library/publication

**What it contains:**
Volume III is the most relevant: it concordances the Taisho against "nine other
Buddhist canons." The ICPBS Library holds Tibetan Kanjur and Tanjur editions,
Pali Tipitaka, and Mongolian Kanjur. If the Tibetan Kanjur/Tengyur is among
the nine canons (which is highly probable given the ICPBS's holdings and
research focus), this would be the most comprehensive systematic
Taisho-to-Tibetan concordance published in Japan.

**Estimated new identifications:** If Volume III includes Tibetan Kanjur and
Tengyur, it could provide 500-1000+ systematic Taisho-Tibetan
correspondences. This is potentially the single most valuable source for our
project. However, I was unable to confirm the exact list of nine canons online.
This requires obtaining a copy of the publication or contacting ICPBS directly.

**Assessment:** Obtaining or consulting Volume III should be a top priority.

## 7. SAT Taisho Database Bibliographic Metadata -- MODERATE PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
SAT Daizokyo Text Database (SAT大正新脩大蔵経テキストデータベース).
Graduate School of Humanities and Sociology, University of Tokyo.
https://21dzk.l.u-tokyo.ac.jp/SAT/

**What it contains:**
The SAT database contains full text of all 85 volumes of the Taisho, plus
bibliographic metadata for each text including: titles in multiple languages
(Japanese, Chinese, Sanskrit, Pali, and Tibetan), translator/author
information, and location references within other canon editions. It is
interoperable with the DDB (Digital Dictionary of Buddhism).

**Digital availability:**
Fully online and searchable at the URL above.

**Estimated new identifications:** The SAT metadata includes Tibetan titles
where known, but the extent of systematic Toh-number cross-referencing in the
metadata is unclear. The database is primarily a full-text search tool, not a
concordance database. However, the bibliographic metadata for each text may
include Tibetan equivalent information that could be programmatically
extracted. Possibly 100-300 identifications depending on metadata completeness.

## 8. Digital Dictionary of Buddhism (DDB) -- MODERATE PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
A. Charles Muller, ed. *Digital Dictionary of Buddhism*.
http://www.buddhism-dict.net/ddb/

**What it contains:**
Over 73,000 entries covering Buddhist terms, texts, schools, temples, and
persons. Text entries include "matching numbers used in the Taisho shinshu
daizokyo edition (T.)" and "listing of numbers for texts in the Complete
Catalogue of the Tibetan Buddhist Canon edited by H. Ui and others at Tohoku
University (To.)."

The DDB explicitly provides Taisho-to-Tohoku cross-references in its text
entries. It is interoperable with the SAT database.

**Digital availability:**
Online at the URL above (requires registration for full access).

**Estimated new identifications:** The DDB contains text entries with both
T-numbers and Toh-numbers. A systematic extraction of all entries with both
fields populated could yield a substantial concordance. However, the DDB is
primarily a dictionary, not a catalogue, so coverage may be uneven. Possibly
200-500 identifications from text entries, with significant overlap with
other sources.

## 9. Kamata Shigeo's Encyclopedic Dictionary -- LOW-MODERATE PRIORITY

Kamata Shigeo (鎌田茂雄), general editor. *Issaikyo kaidai jiten*
(一切経解題辞典). Tokyo: Daito Shuppansha (大東出版社), 2002.

**What it contains:**
An encyclopedic dictionary of all Buddhist texts in the canon, with entries

**Full citation:**

A comprehensive explanatory dictionary of all texts in the Taisho Daizokyo.
Likely includes Tibetan parallel references where known.

**Estimated new identifications (both works combined):** These reference
works synthesize existing scholarship rather than providing new
identifications. Their value is in confirming and consolidating known
correspondences. Possibly 20-50 identifications not captured elsewhere.

## 10. Lu Cheng's New Catalogue -- MODERATE PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
Lu Cheng (呂澂). *Xinbian Hanwen Dazangjing mulu*
(新編漢文大藏經目録; New Catalogue of the Chinese Buddhist Canon).

**What it contains:**
Lu Cheng (1896-1989) was one of the earliest modern Chinese scholars to have
studied Sanskrit, Pali, and classical Tibetan. His multilingual expertise
enabled him to incorporate philological methods from all three traditions into
his cataloguing work. His *Xinbian Hanwen Dazangjing mulu* represents a
comprehensive re-cataloguing of the Chinese Buddhist canon with systematic
attention to Indian and Tibetan parallels.

**Estimated new identifications:** As a major Chinese scholar with Tibetan
language competence, Lu Cheng's catalogue likely contains Chinese-Tibetan
correspondences drawn from his own comparative work. However, much of this may
now be superseded by later digital resources. Possibly 30-100 identifications,
with value primarily for texts where Lu Cheng had unique scholarly insight.

## 11. rKTs GitHub Data -- HIGH PRIORITY (already partially covered)

Bruno Grunagel et al. *Resources for Kanjur & Tanjur Studies (rKTs)*.
Tibetan Manuscript Project Vienna, University of Vienna.
GitHub: https://github.com/brunogml/rKTs
Website: http://www.rkts.org/

**What it contains:**
We already use rKTs, but the GitHub repository contains structured XML data
with explicit `<cmp>` (comparison) elements, including a `translation-taisho`
field that "indicates the text is a translation from Chinese, with the Taisho
number of the Chinese text provided." This structured data may contain
additional Taisho cross-references beyond what we've extracted from the web
interface and Otani concordance.

**Estimated new identifications:** We should verify that our existing rKTs
extraction captures all `translation-taisho` tagged entries. The GitHub data
may reveal additional entries not visible through the web interface alone.
Possibly 20-50 additional identifications.

## 12. MITRA Project (UC Berkeley) -- FUTURE RESOURCE

**Full citation:**
Kurt Keutzer et al. "MITRA: A Large-Scale Parallel Corpus and Multilingual
Pretrained Language Model for Machine Translation and Semantic Retrieval for
Pali, Sanskrit, Buddhist Chinese, and Tibetan." arXiv:2601.06400, January 2026.

**What it contains:**
The MITRA framework includes a corpus of 1.74 million parallel sentence pairs
between Sanskrit, Chinese, and Tibetan, with machine learning models for
cross-lingual alignment. While this is primarily a computational linguistics
project, the underlying parallel corpus implies systematic identification of
text-level correspondences across canons.

**Estimated new identifications:** The MITRA corpus is focused on
sentence-level alignment rather than catalogue-level concordance. However, the
text pairs underlying the corpus implicitly define text-level correspondences.
The project is still in development and may produce concordance data as a
byproduct. Worth monitoring but not immediately actionable.

## 14. Nanjio Catalogue (1883) -- LOW PRIORITY (historical)

**Full citation:**
Nanjio Bunyiu (南条文雄). *A Catalogue of the Chinese Translation of the
Buddhist Tripitaka, the Sacred Canon of the Buddhists in China and Japan*.
Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1883.

Available on Internet Archive:
https://archive.org/details/acataloguechine00nanjgoog

**What it contains:**
The Nanjio catalogue predates both the Taisho and the Tohoku catalogues.
It uses its own numbering system (N-numbers). It is primarily useful as a
bridge: the Hobogirin Repertoire and other later works use Nanjio numbers
alongside Taisho numbers, and the Tohoku catalogue uses Nanjio numbers for
Chinese equivalents.

**Estimated new identifications:** None directly, but the Nanjio-to-Taisho
concordance is essential for converting references in the Tohoku catalogue
(which uses Nanjio numbers) to T-numbers.

## 15. Akanuma Chizen's Comparative Catalogue -- LOW PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
Akanuma Chizen (赤沼智善). *Kanpa shibu shiagon goshoroku*
(漢巴四部四阿含互照録; Comparative Catalogue of Chinese Agamas and Pali
Nikayas). Nagoya: Hajinkaku Shobo, 1929. 424 pp.
Reprinted: Sankinbo Busshorin, 1958; Sri Satguru Publications, 1990.

**What it contains:**
A systematic concordance between Chinese Agama texts and Pali Nikaya texts.
This does not directly address Tibetan parallels, but the Agama texts in the
Taisho have Tibetan parallels in many cases, and this concordance helps
identify the network of parallels across all three traditions.

**Estimated new identifications:** None directly for Taisho-Tibetan, but
useful as a secondary reference.

## 17. DharmaNexus / BuddhaNexus -- MODERATE-HIGH PRIORITY (computational)

**Full citation:**
Sebastian Nehrdich, Orna Almogi, and Dorji Wangchuk. *BuddhaNexus* (2019-2023),
now continued as *DharmaNexus* (2023-present). Originally Khyentse Center for
Tibetan Buddhist Textual Scholarship, University of Hamburg; now Dharmamitra
platform, UC Berkeley / Tohoku University.

- Legacy: https://buddhanexus.net (deprecated)
- Current: https://dharmamitra.org/nexus
- GitHub: https://github.com/BuddhaNexus/
- Documentation: https://dharmamitra.github.io/dharmamitra-guides/dharmanexus

**What it contains:**
DharmaNexus is an intertextuality discovery platform for Pali, Sanskrit,
Chinese, and Tibetan Buddhist texts. It uses both token-based matching and
deep semantic similarity (Gemma 2 MITRA-E model) to find parallel passages
across and within languages. Users can navigate using standard numbering
systems (Taisho numbers, Derge folio numbers, SuttaCentral IDs). The platform
hosts segmented text corpora for all four languages.

The GitHub organization contains segmented input files:
- `BuddhaNexus/segmented-chinese`: Segmented Chinese text files (CBETA)
- `BuddhaNexus/segmented-tibetan`: Segmented Tibetan text files (ACIP, BDRC)

The match data itself is too large for GitHub and must be hosted separately.

**Digital availability:**
Fully online at dharmamitra.org/nexus. The team provides API access for
collaborating institutions and is willing to share data resources. Sebastian
Nehrdich (Distinguished Assistant Professor at Tohoku University) is the
primary contact.

**Assessment:**
(a) Accessible: Yes, web interface is public. API access available on request.
(b) Structured Taisho-Tohoku mappings: Not directly. DharmaNexus provides
    passage-level intertextuality, not catalogue-level concordance. One would
    need to aggregate passage matches to infer text-level correspondences.
(c) New parallels: Potentially significant. The multilingual matching could
    identify Chinese-Tibetan text pairs that have not been catalogued in
    traditional concordances. However, extracting structured concordance data
    from passage-level matches would require substantial post-processing.

**Estimated new identifications:** Unknown; depends on match aggregation.
The computational approach could in principle discover correspondences missed
by manual cataloguing, but the output is not in concordance format. Worth
contacting Nehrdich directly to ask whether text-level Taisho-Tohoku
correspondence data has been extracted as a byproduct.

## 18. MITRA Parallel Corpus (detailed) -- MODERATE PRIORITY (data resource)

**Full citation:**
Sebastian Nehrdich et al. "MITRA: A Large-Scale Parallel Corpus and
Multilingual Pretrained Language Model for Machine Translation and Semantic
Retrieval for Pali, Sanskrit, Buddhist Chinese, and Tibetan."
arXiv:2601.06400, January 2026.

- Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.06400
- Data: https://github.com/dharmamitra/mitra-parallel
- License: Permissive (code); Creative Commons (data)

**What it contains:**
The mitra-parallel repository contains 1.74 million parallel sentence pairs
across Sanskrit, Buddhist Chinese, and Tibetan, stored as TSV files. Each
record includes source segment identifier, source sentence, target segment
identifier, and target sentence. The segment identifiers reference canonical
text locations (Taisho text/page for Chinese, Derge folio for Tibetan).

The Chinese texts derive from the CBETA corpus (Taisho edition), and the
Tibetan texts from ACIP, BDRC, and Tsadra Foundation collections.

**Assessment:**
(a) Accessible: Yes, public GitHub repository, downloadable.
(b) Structured Taisho-Tohoku mappings: Implicitly, yes. The sentence pairs
    link specific Taisho text segments to specific Tibetan text segments. By
    aggregating segment identifiers, one could extract which Taisho texts have
    sentence-level parallels with which Tibetan texts.
(c) New parallels: Potentially many. At 1.74 million pairs, this corpus
    encompasses a large fraction of the parallel Buddhist literature. However,
    the parallels are automatically generated (not manually verified) and may
    include false positives.

**Estimated new identifications:** Could yield 100-500+ text-level
correspondences after aggregation and filtering, but with uncertain precision.
The segment identifiers need to be parsed to extract T-numbers and Toh-numbers.

## 19. DILA / DDBC Authority Database (Catalog Module) -- MODERATE-HIGH PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
Dharma Drum Institute of Liberal Arts (DILA). *Buddhist Studies Authority
Database Project* (佛學規範資料庫).
- Portal: https://authority.dila.edu.tw/
- Catalog: https://authority.dila.edu.tw/catalog
- API docs: https://authority.dila.edu.tw/docs/services/
- GitHub: https://github.com/DILA-edu/Authority-Databases
- Open data: https://authority.dila.edu.tw/docs/open_content/download.php
- Table schemas: https://authority.dila.edu.tw/docs/open_content/table_schemas.php

**What it contains:**
The DILA Authority Database includes four modules: Person, Place, Time, and
Catalog. The Catalog module (佛教藏經目錄數位資料庫, Digital Database of
Buddhist Tripitaka Catalogues) contains:
- 22 editions, approximately 33,700 catalog entries from Chinese Tripitaka
- Approximately 4,569 entries from the Tibetan Tripitaka
- 7,003 entries from the Pali Tipitaka (Nikaya and Agama suttas)

The database uses TEI markup and is designed to cross-reference texts across
different Tripitaka editions and traditions. It provides a JSON API with no
rate limits.

The GitHub repository provides data as XML/TEI files under CC BY-SA 3.0.
The `authority_catalog/` directory exists but its contents are not fully
documented in the README. The Open Content download page provides SQL and CSV
exports.

**Assessment:**
(a) Accessible: Yes, both API and downloadable data.
(b) Structured Taisho-Tohoku mappings: Very likely. The catalog module
    explicitly covers both Chinese (including Taisho) and Tibetan editions.
    The cross-referencing across 22 editions is the core purpose. The API
    endpoint and table schemas need investigation to confirm which fields
    link Taisho to Tohoku numbers.
(c) New parallels: Potentially significant. With 33,700 Chinese and 4,569
    Tibetan catalog entries, the database may contain cross-references we
    have not yet extracted. The overlap with CBETA Jinglu (which DILA also
    hosts) may be high, but the structured API access could reveal data not
    available through the Jinglu web scraping.

**Estimated new identifications:** Possibly 50-200, depending on overlap with
CBETA Jinglu. The API access is the key advantage: programmatic extraction
of all Taisho-Tibetan links in the catalog database could be done efficiently.

**Action:** Query the DILA catalog API to enumerate all catalog entries that
have both a Taisho identifier and a Tibetan (Tohoku/Peking) identifier. Contact
winxd@dila.edu.tw for documentation on the catalog module specifically.

## 20. CBETA API and Metadata Repository -- MODERATE PRIORITY

**Full citation:**
CBETA Data Service Center (CBETA 數位研究平台).
- Data portal: https://cbdata.dila.edu.tw/
- API: Ruby on Rails, e.g. `/v1.2/works?work=T0001`

## 23. Felbur, Meelen, and Vierthaler Dataset -- MODERATE PRIORITY (computational)

**Full citation:**
Rafal Felbur, Marieke Meelen, and Paul Vierthaler. "Crosslinguistic Semantic
Textual Similarity of Buddhist Chinese and Classical Tibetan." *Journal of
Open Humanities Data* 8, article 23 (2022).

- Paper: https://openhumanitiesdata.metajnl.com/articles/10.5334/johd.86
- Code: https://github.com/vierth/buddhist_chinese_classical_tibetan
- Data (Chinese embeddings): https://zenodo.org/records/6782932
- Data (Tibetan embeddings): https://zenodo.org/records/6782247
- Alignment guidelines: https://zenodo.org/records/6782150

**What it contains:**
The first published procedure for identifying highly similar sequences of
text in Chinese and Tibetan translations of Buddhist sutra literature, using
cross-lingual word embeddings (FastText). The Chinese corpus is drawn from
the CBETA/Kanseki repository (Taisho edition). The Tibetan corpus uses the
Derge eKangyur and eTengyur. The method produces unsupervised parallel
alignments at word, sentence, and paragraph level.

Produced by the Open Philology project (ERC-funded, Leiden University,
2018-2022).

**Assessment:**
(a) Accessible: Yes, all code and data on GitHub and Zenodo under open
    licenses.
(b) Structured Taisho-Tohoku mappings: Not directly. The output is
    similarity scores between text segments, not a concordance table. However,
    the underlying methodology could be applied to produce text-level
    correspondences.
(c) New parallels: The embedding models are general-purpose tools. The
    researchers demonstrated the method but did not publish a comprehensive
    concordance. The primary value is methodological: their approach could be
    used to computationally identify Taisho-Tibetan parallels.

**Estimated new identifications:** Not directly applicable without additional
computation. The embeddings and alignment code are available to run.

## Appendix: The Taisho Editorial Apparatus and Tibetan References

A key question is whether the Taisho Shinshu Daizokyo (1922-1934) itself
included Tibetan cross-references in its original editorial apparatus. The
answer is nuanced:

1. **The main Taisho text (vols. 1-85):** The critical apparatus (footnotes)
   collates Chinese editions against each other (Song, Yuan, Ming, Korean,
   Fangshan stone, etc.) but does not systematically reference Tibetan parallels.

2. **The Showa Hobo Somokuroku (vols. 98-100, 1929-1934):** Reprints all
   extant Chinese sutra catalogues and provides cross-edition concordance tables.
   These tables are Chinese-to-Chinese (mapping across different Chinese canon
   editions) and do not include Tibetan references.

3. **The Hobogirin Fascicule Annexe (1931):** Published as a companion index to
   the Taisho by the same team (Takakusu was involved in both projects). The
   1931 edition included Sanskrit/Pali titles but predated the Tohoku catalogue
   (1934). The 1978 revised edition by Demieville, Durt, and Seidel incorporated
   Tibetan references marked with a double-cross symbol.

4. **Takakusu and Watanabe's editorial intent:** The editors were trained in
   European philological methods and aware of the Tibetan parallel tradition, but
   the Taisho project was primarily focused on establishing a critical text of the
   Chinese canon. Tibetan cross-referencing was considered a separate scholarly
   enterprise, which was partly carried out by the Tohoku team (Ui Hakuju et al.)
   and later by the Hobogirin project.

In summary, the Taisho itself does not contain systematic Tibetan cross-
references. The Hobogirin Repertoire (especially the 1978 edition) is the
closest thing to an official Taisho-to-Tibetan concordance produced by
scholars associated with the Taisho project.
