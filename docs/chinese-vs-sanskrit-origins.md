# Chinese Composition vs. Sanskrit Origin: Digest Sutras in the Taisho Canon

## 1. The Scholarly Debate

One of the most consequential questions in Buddhist textual scholarship is whether
certain short sutras in the Chinese canon are genuine translations from Indic
originals or were composed directly in Chinese. This question has profound
implications for our understanding of Buddhist textual history, the development of
Mahayana thought, and the creative role of Chinese Buddhist communities in
producing scripture.

The debate spans a spectrum from texts universally acknowledged as Chinese
compositions (e.g., the *Sutra of Perfect Enlightenment*, T842) to texts whose
status remains genuinely uncertain. At the center of this debate lies a category
of particular interest to our project: **digest sutras** -- short texts that
condense the content of longer works. Were these digests extracted from Chinese
translations (implying Chinese authorship), or do they reflect independent Indic
condensation traditions that were subsequently translated into Chinese?

### 1.1 Jan Nattier and the Heart Sutra

The most influential modern argument for Chinese composition of a canonical text
is Jan Nattier's 1992 article "The Heart Sutra: A Chinese Apocryphal Text?"
(published in the *Journal of the International Association of Buddhist Studies*
15.2). Nattier argued that the *Heart Sutra* (specifically the "short text" form
represented by T250 and T251) was not translated from Sanskrit but was composed
in Chinese by extracting passages from Kumarajiva's Chinese translation of the
*Large Perfection of Wisdom Sutra* (T223, *Mohe bore boluomi jing*), and that the
Sanskrit version was a subsequent **back-translation** from the Chinese.

Nattier's core evidence included:

1. **Verbatim correspondence with a specific Chinese translation.** The text of
   T250 (attributed to Kumarajiva) matches T223 (also by Kumarajiva) virtually
   word-for-word in its doctrinal core. This is the kind of match one would
   expect if someone extracted passages directly from the Chinese translation, not
   if two translators independently rendered the same Sanskrit into Chinese.

2. **Translation anomalies in the Sanskrit.** The extant Sanskrit *Heart Sutra*
   contains passages that read like back-translations from Chinese, including
   non-standard Sanskrit constructions that correspond naturally to Chinese
   phrasing but are unusual in Indic Prajnaparamita literature.

3. **The "missing" nidana and conclusion.** The short form of the *Heart Sutra*
   lacks the standard sutra opening ("Thus have I heard...") and closing, which
   is characteristic of Chinese excerpt texts (chao jing 鈔經) but unusual for an
   independently circulated Indic sutra.

4. **Catalog evidence.** The earliest Chinese scripture catalogs list the *Heart
   Sutra* under categories that suggest uncertainty about its provenance. The
   attribution to Kumarajiva is contested; the text does not appear in early
   catalogs of his translations.

Nattier's argument has been widely influential but not universally accepted.
Scholars including Fukui Fumimasa, Tanahashi Kazuaki, and more recently Jonathan
Silk have offered various responses. Key counter-arguments include:

- The Gilgit manuscript and other Sanskrit manuscripts demonstrate that the *Heart
  Sutra* circulated in Sanskrit in Central Asia, possibly quite early.
- The "long form" of the *Heart Sutra* (with nidana and conclusion) exists in both
  Sanskrit and in later Chinese translations (T253, T254, T255, T256, T257),
  suggesting an Indic textual tradition.
- Back-translation from Chinese to Sanskrit, while not impossible, would be a
  highly unusual event in the history of Buddhist textual transmission.
- Matthew Orsborn (Huifeng) has argued (2017) for a more nuanced position where
  the core of the text may derive from an Indic Prajnaparamita tradition, even if
  the specific short form was shaped by Chinese editorial practices.

### 1.2 Chinese Buddhist Apocrypha (偽經 weijing / 疑經 yijing)

The *Heart Sutra* debate sits within a much larger scholarly tradition of
identifying "apocryphal" or "suspect" texts in the Chinese Buddhist canon. The
Chinese bibliographic tradition itself developed sophisticated methods for
classifying texts:

- **偽經 weijing** ("spurious sutras"): Texts identified as not being genuine
  translations from Indic originals.
- **疑經 yijing** ("suspect sutras"): Texts whose provenance is uncertain.

The phenomenon was recognized from at least the fifth century. The great
bibliographer Sengyou (僧祐, 445-518) in his *Chu sanzang ji ji* (出三藏記集, T2145)
created categories for texts of doubtful origin. Later catalogs by Fei Changfang
(費長房, *Lidai sanbao ji* 歷代三寶紀, T2034, 597 CE), Daoxuan (道宣, *Da Tang neidian
lu* 大唐內典錄, T2149, 664 CE), and most importantly Zhisheng (智昇, *Kaiyuan shijiao
lu* 開元釋教錄, T2154, 730 CE) refined these classifications.

Robert Buswell's important collection *Chinese Buddhist Apocrypha* (1990) and
Kyoko Tokuno's work established that Chinese-composed sutras were not mere
"forgeries" but represented a vital creative tradition. These texts were produced
by Chinese monks who believed they were channeling authentic Buddhist teaching,
even if they composed the texts in Chinese rather than translating from an Indic
source.

### 1.3 The Role of Scripture Catalogs (經錄 jinglu)

The Chinese bibliographic tradition provides crucial evidence for provenance
questions. Key indicators used by catalog compilers included:

- **Translator attribution**: Texts lacking a named translator or with
  questionable attributions were flagged. Many digest texts are attributed to
  famous translators like Kumarajiva or Zhu Fahu (Dharmaraksha) but appear in
  catalogs only centuries after the alleged translator's death.

- **Attestation history**: When a text first appears in a catalog provides a
  *terminus ante quem*. If a sutra attributed to a second-century translator
  first appears in a seventh-century catalog, suspicion is warranted.

- **Consistency with known translation practices**: Each major translator had
  recognizable stylistic habits. A text attributed to Kumarajiva that lacks his
  characteristic phrasing may not be genuine.

- **Presence of Chinese-specific content**: References to Chinese customs, five
  phases cosmology, or Confucian/Daoist concepts absent from Indian Buddhism
  suggest Chinese composition.

## 2. Criteria for Identifying Chinese-Composed Texts

Modern scholars have refined the traditional catalog criteria into a more
systematic framework. The key indicators include:

### 2.1 Linguistic Markers

**Vocabulary and syntax**: Genuine translations from Sanskrit and other Indic
languages tend to preserve certain syntactic patterns reflecting the source
language, including:

- Longer compound nouns (reflecting Sanskrit compounds)
- Particular patterns of negation (reflecting Sanskrit nominal negation with *a-/an-*)
- Standard translation equivalents for technical terms that varied by period and
  translator school
- Formulaic passages (e.g., sutra openings, merit transfer passages) that follow
  established translation conventions

Chinese-composed texts, by contrast, tend to show:

- More natural Chinese word order and syntax
- Mixing of technical terminology from different translation traditions
- Incorporation of Daoist or Confucian vocabulary
- Four-character phrasing (四字句) more characteristic of literary Chinese than
  of translation style

**Translator fingerprints**: Major translators like Kumarajiva, Xuanzang,
Dharmaraksha, and Paramartha each had distinctive translation vocabularies.
Erik Zurcher, Jan Nattier, and others have cataloged these. A text attributed
to Kumarajiva that uses Xuanzang's terminology is suspect.

### 2.2 Structural Markers

- **Missing or aberrant nidana/conclusion**: Authentic translations typically
  preserve the full sutra frame. Chinese compositions sometimes omit these or
  use non-standard formulations.

- **Inconsistent genre signals**: A text that mixes elements from different
  Buddhist genres (sutra, vinaya, abhidharma, dharani) in ways not found in
  Indic literature.

- **Length and completeness**: Unusually short texts that appear to be excerpts
  from longer works, particularly when the excerpting follows the text of a
  specific known Chinese translation.

### 2.3 The Digest Criterion (Central to Our Project)

The most directly relevant criterion for our pipeline is what we might call the
**translator-specific verbatim matching** test:

> If a short text (the "digest") matches the specific **Chinese wording** of a
> particular translator's version of a longer text -- not just the doctrinal
> content that any translation would share -- then the digest was likely created
> by extracting from that Chinese translation, rather than being independently
> translated from an Indic condensation.

This is precisely Nattier's argument about the *Heart Sutra*: T250 does not
merely share the doctrinal content of T223 (which any translation of the
Prajnaparamita would share); it shares Kumarajiva's **specific Chinese
phrasing** from T223.

**Multi-source digest analysis for T250:**

T08n0250 has a **combined coverage of 95.6%** when considering multiple sources

existing Chinese Prajnaparamita translations.

### 3.3 Interpretation

These numbers are strikingly consistent with Nattier's hypothesis:

1. **Same-translator matching is dramatically higher.** T250 (attributed to
   Kumarajiva) matches Kumarajiva's T223 at **73.2% coverage**, while T251
   (Xuanzang) matches the same T223 at only **44.6%**. This 28.6 percentage
   point gap is exactly what we would expect if T250 was composed by extracting
   from T223's Chinese text: the match is at the level of specific Chinese
   wording, not just shared doctrinal content.

2. **Xuanzang's version matches Xuanzang's Prajna better.** T251 matches T07n0220
   (Xuanzang's translation of the Large Prajna, fasc. 401-600) at **55.4%**,
   higher than its match with Kumarajiva's T223 (44.6%). This suggests that
   Xuanzang's *Heart Sutra* translation, while sharing a textual tradition with
   T250, was made to align with Xuanzang's own translation vocabulary -- or
   potentially was also composed/edited with reference to his own Large Prajna
   translation.

3. **Cross-translator overlap is lower but not negligible.** The fact that
   different Heart Sutra translations still share 43-65% with each other reflects
   the shared doctrinal core -- all versions convey the same Prajnaparamita
   teachings. But the **differential** between same-translator matching (73%)
   and cross-translator matching (44-55%) is the computational signature of
   Chinese-level composition.

4. **Average segment length is telling.** T250's match with T223 shows an average
   segment length of only 27.25 characters across 8 source regions with a longest
   segment of 156 characters. This is consistent with the text being assembled
   from multiple extracted passages, not copied as a single continuous block.
   Compare this with T251 vs. T223, which has an average segment length of only
   12.89 characters -- much shorter segments, consistent with content overlap
   rather than verbatim extraction.

### 3.4 Broader Patterns in Our 2,812 Relationships

Beyond the Heart Sutra, our dataset reveals several patterns relevant to the
Chinese-composition question:

**Encyclopedia digests (法苑珠林 T2122, 經律異相 T2121, 諸經要集 T2123):**
These are *known* Chinese compilations that extracted material from canonical
texts. They provide a control group -- we know these are Chinese compositions,
and they show extremely high digest coverage (often 80-100%) with their sources.
For example:

- T02n0115 as digest of T2122: 98.3% coverage, confidence 0.783
- T15n0615 as digest of T2122: 100% coverage, confidence 0.750
- T14n0503 as digest of T02n0099: 100% coverage, confidence 0.750

These known Chinese extracts show the pattern we would expect: very high coverage,
with the extracted text being almost entirely contained in the source.

**Dharani collections (陀羅尼雜集 T1336):**
Multiple short dharani texts show 100% coverage as digests of the dharani
collection, suggesting they were extracted from the Chinese compilation rather
than independently translated. This is consistent with the known practice of
extracting individual dharani texts from larger collections for ritual use.

**Vinaya digests (戒本 texts from 四分律 T1428):**
Precept texts extracted from the *Four-Part Vinaya* show high coverage (65-81%)
as digests. These are known to be Chinese extractions for monastic use, providing
another calibration point.

## 4. A Concrete Research Plan

### 4.1 The Core Hypothesis

We can state the testable hypothesis as follows:

> **If a digest text was composed in Chinese by extracting from an existing
> Chinese translation, it should show significantly higher verbatim overlap with
> that specific translator's version than with other Chinese translations of the
> same underlying Indic text.**

Conversely:

> **If a digest text was independently translated from an Indic condensation, it
> should show roughly equal overlap with all Chinese translations of the parent
> text, because the overlap would be at the level of shared content, not
> translator-specific phrasing.**

### 4.2 Analysis 1: Translator-Specific Matching Differentials

**Method:** For each digest relationship where the source text exists in multiple
Chinese translations (retranslation pairs), compute the **matching differential**:
the difference in coverage between the best-matching translation and the
next-best-matching translation by a different translator.

**Prediction:**
- Chinese-composed digests will show a **large differential** (high match with one
  translator, lower match with others).
- Genuinely translated digests will show a **small differential** (similar match
  levels across different translations of the same source).

**Implementation with existing pipeline:**

1. From our 288 retranslation pairs, identify groups of texts that are different
   Chinese translations of the same underlying work.

2. For each digest that matches one member of a retranslation group, compute its
   coverage against *all* members of that group.

3. Calculate the differential: `max_coverage - mean_other_coverage`.

4. A large differential (e.g., >15 percentage points) suggests Chinese-level
   composition; a small differential suggests translation from an independent
   Indic source.

**Application to Heart Sutra:**
Our data already shows: T250 vs. T223 (same translator, Kumarajiva) = 73.2%,
while T251 vs. T223 (cross-translator) = 44.6%. Differential = 28.6 points.
This is a very large differential, strongly supporting Chinese composition.

### 4.3 Analysis 2: Segment Length Distributions

**Method:** Compare the average and maximum segment lengths for digest
relationships, distinguishing between known Chinese extractions and texts of
uncertain provenance.

**Prediction:**
- Chinese extractions should show longer verbatim segments (direct copying).
- Texts translated from a shared Indic source should show shorter matching
  segments (overlapping content expressed in different Chinese phrasing).

**Implementation:** Our pipeline already computes `avg_segment_length` and
`longest_segment` for every relationship. We can:

1. Establish the distribution for known Chinese extractions (encyclopedia digests,
   vinaya extractions).
2. Establish the distribution for known independent translations of related texts
   (retranslation pairs).
3. Classify uncertain texts based on which distribution their segment lengths
   match.

**Heart Sutra application:** T250 vs. T223 has avg_segment_length=27.25 and
longest_segment=156. T251 vs. T223 has avg_segment_length=12.89 and
longest_segment=62. The T250 pattern is closer to known Chinese extractions; the
T251 pattern is closer to retranslation overlap.

### 4.4 Analysis 3: Novel Fraction Analysis

**Method:** Examine the `novel_fraction` -- the proportion of the digest text
that has no match in the source. Genuinely translated digests might contain
translator-specific framing material (introductions, connecting prose) that
would be novel. Chinese extractions would have lower novel fractions.

**Implementation:** Compare novel fractions across categories:
- T250 vs. T223: novel_fraction = 0.2685 (about 27% novel)
- T251 vs. T223: novel_fraction = 0.5538 (about 55% novel)

The higher novel fraction for T251 could reflect either (a) Xuanzang used
different Chinese phrasing from Kumarajiva, resulting in less verbatim overlap,
or (b) T251 contains more material not drawn from the Large Prajna (e.g., the
mantra section, the framing of the short form). Both interpretations support
the idea that T250 was composed with direct reference to T223's Chinese text.

### 4.5 Analysis 4: Source Span Patterns

**Method:** Our pipeline computes `source_span` -- the proportion of the source
text that the matched regions cover. A digest that extracts from scattered
locations across a large text will have a different source span profile than one
that tracks a single continuous section.

**Prediction:** Chinese-composed digests selecting "greatest hits" passages from a
long sutra should show small source spans (concentrated extraction from specific
passages). This is exactly what we see: T250 vs. T223 has source_span = 0.000776
(the matched material comes from a tiny fraction of the 285,000+ character T223),
consistent with selective extraction of key Prajnaparamita passages.

### 4.6 Analysis 5: Systematic Catalog Cross-Referencing

**Method:** Cross-reference our computationally detected digest relationships
with the classification status of texts in traditional Chinese catalogs
(particularly Zhisheng's *Kaiyuan shijiao lu*, T2154).

**Implementation:**

1. From our 132 excerpt and 533 digest relationships, identify the digest texts.
2. Cross-reference each with catalog data to determine whether it is classified
   as:
   - Authentic translation with named translator
   - Anonymous translation (失譯 shiyi)
   - Suspect text (疑經 yijing)
   - Spurious text (偽經 weijing)
3. Compute whether computationally detected digests are disproportionately
   represented in the suspect/spurious categories.

**Prediction:** We expect a significant correlation between texts showing high
same-translator matching differentials and texts flagged in catalogs as suspect
or spurious.

### 4.7 Analysis 6: Cross-Translator Matrix for Major Text Families

**Method:** For major text families with multiple Chinese translations (the
Prajnaparamita, the Lotus Sutra, the Nirvana Sutra, the Avatamsaka), build a
full matrix of digest/retranslation relationships and examine whether digest
texts preferentially match one translator's version.

**Example -- Prajnaparamita family:**

We already have rich data for this family. Our pipeline detected relationships
between the Heart Sutra versions and multiple Prajnaparamita texts:

- T08n0250 (attr. Kumarajiva Heart Sutra) vs. T08n0223 (Kumarajiva Prajna): 73.2%
- T08n0250 vs. T25n1509 (Dazhidu lun, Kumarajiva tradition): 72.8%
- T08n0250 vs. T07n0220 (Xuanzang Prajna): 51.0%
- T08n0251 (Xuanzang Heart Sutra) vs. T07n0220 (Xuanzang Prajna): 55.4%
- T08n0251 vs. T08n0223 (Kumarajiva Prajna): 44.6%

The pattern is clear: each Heart Sutra version matches its "own" translator's
Prajna more closely than the other translator's version. This is the
computational fingerprint of Chinese-level composition.

We should extend this analysis to every text family where multiple translations
exist, systematically searching for this translator-specific matching signature
across the entire canon.

### 4.8 Analysis 7: The Dharani Test

**Method:** Many short dharani sutras are suspected of being Chinese extractions
from larger dharani collections. Our data shows numerous such relationships (e.g.,
16 texts identified as digests of T21n1336 *Tuoluoni za ji*). We can use these as
a test case where the direction of extraction is clear (the collection was
compiled first, individual texts were extracted for ritual use), validating our
methodology before applying it to more ambiguous cases.

## 5. Implications and Limitations

### 5.1 What Computational Analysis Can and Cannot Show

Our pipeline can demonstrate:

- **Verbatim textual dependence** between specific Chinese texts
- **Translator-specific matching patterns** that distinguish Chinese extraction
  from independent translation
- **Statistical profiles** (segment lengths, coverage, novel fractions) that
  characterize different types of textual relationships

Our pipeline **cannot** directly determine:

- **Direction of dependence** in all cases (though length asymmetry is a strong
  signal)
- **Whether an Indic original once existed** but is now lost
- **Intentionality** -- whether a Chinese author deliberately composed a text or
  whether it emerged through gradual editorial processes
- **Dating** -- when the textual relationship was established

### 5.2 The Spectrum of Possibilities

For any given digest relationship, the reality may not be a simple binary
(Chinese vs. Indic). Possible scenarios include:

1. **Pure Chinese composition**: A Chinese author excerpted passages from an
   existing Chinese translation and composed a new text.

2. **Chinese editing of an Indic text**: A translator worked from an Indic source
   but also consulted and incorporated phrasing from an existing Chinese
   translation of a related text.

3. **Indic condensation, subsequently harmonized**: An authentic Indic condensation
   was translated into Chinese, but a later editor revised the Chinese to align
   with an existing Chinese translation of the parent text.

4. **Independent Indic condensation**: The digest was independently composed in an
   Indic language and independently translated into Chinese, with textual overlap
   reflecting shared doctrinal content.

Our computational analysis can distinguish scenarios 1 and 4 with reasonable
confidence (high vs. low translator-specific differentials). Scenarios 2 and 3
are harder to distinguish from scenario 1 computationally, but the segment length
and coverage profiles may provide clues.

### 5.3 Scale of the Question

Of our 2,812 detected relationships, the 132 excerpts and 533 digests
are the primary candidates for this analysis. Among these, cases where the source
text exists in multiple Chinese translations by different translators provide the
most diagnostic power. Our 288 retranslation pairs identify these multi-translation
text families. The intersection of these two sets -- digest texts whose sources
also exist as retranslation groups -- is where our methodology will be most
powerful.

## 6. Conclusion

The question of Chinese composition vs. Indic origin is not merely an academic
curiosity. It bears on fundamental questions about how Buddhist texts were
produced, transmitted, and transformed as they moved across linguistic and
cultural boundaries. Our computational pipeline, designed to detect digest
relationships through character-level alignment, is uniquely positioned to
contribute to this question because it can detect the **translator-specific
verbatim matching** that is the strongest evidence for Chinese-level composition.

The Heart Sutra case study, where our pipeline independently confirms Nattier's
philological argument through computational means, demonstrates the potential of
this approach. Extending it systematically across the 665 digest relationships
(excerpts + digests) in our dataset could identify previously unrecognized cases of
Chinese composition and contribute new quantitative evidence to longstanding
debates about textual authenticity in the Chinese Buddhist canon.
